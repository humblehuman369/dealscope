## LinkedIn — DealGapIQ company

*~2,100 chars. Hook is the contrarian observation.*

> Most investors who lose Subject-To deals don't lose them on the math.
>
> They lose them on the call.
>
> You know what Sub2 is. You know it solves the rate problem. You know the seller has zero equity and needs to walk. The math is right there in front of you. And then the seller picks up the phone, you say "have you considered a Subject-To structure?" and the call ends.
>
> Three lines that kill the pitch in the first 10 seconds — every Sub2 newcomer says at least one of them:
>
> ❌ "Have you considered a Subject-To structure?" → cold-opens a technical term before the seller has any context
>
> ❌ "There's a Due-on-Sale clause but it's rarely enforced." → you created the worry and dismissed it in the same sentence
>
> ❌ "This is creative financing, but it's totally legal." → defending a position the seller hadn't questioned
>
> The pattern: defending against an objection the seller hasn't raised yet.
>
> The script that lands has 5 parts:
>
> 1️⃣ Open with what they keep, not what you take ("I can pay full asking — no haircut on price.")
> 2️⃣ Translate the structure into seller-language ("the existing loan stays in place at the bank — I take title, I make the payments")
> 3️⃣ Anchor on the seller's win — three concrete reasons ("faster close. No appraisal contingency. No financing fall-through.")
> 4️⃣ Name the structure once, briefly ("This is what's called a Subject-To. Title companies handle it routinely.")
> 5️⃣ End with a soft question, not a hard close ("What does the timing look like on your end?")
>
> The 5-part template, three variants by seller type (motivated / owner-occupant / tired landlord), and the full annotated script — link in comments.
>
> The price tag isn't the deal. The structure is.

## X / Twitter — Thread (7 tweets)

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
> Three lines that kill a Subject-To pitch in the first 10 seconds.
>
> Every Sub2 newcomer says at least one. Every closed Sub2 deal avoided all three. 🧵

**Tweet 2:**
> ❌ Killer #1: "Have you considered a Subject-To structure?"
>
> Cold-opens the technical term before the seller has context. Sellers don't speak investor — and "subject to" sounds like there's a contingency.
>
> Use the term in step 4, not step 1.

**Tweet 3:**
> ❌ Killer #2: "There's a Due-on-Sale clause but it's rarely enforced."
>
> You created the worry and dismissed it in the same sentence. The seller hears the worry, not the dismissal.
>
> If the topic comes up, address it after the structure is on the table.

**Tweet 4:**
> ❌ Killer #3: "This is creative financing, but it's totally legal."
>
> The phrase "but it's legal" makes the seller wonder why you're saying it.
>
> Sub2 is legal. Don't pre-defend it. Let the title attorney's involvement signal legitimacy.

**Tweet 5:**
> The fix: 5-part script structure.
>
> 1. Open with what they keep
> 2. Translate the structure into seller-language
> 3. Anchor on the seller's win (3 reasons)
> 4. Name the structure once, briefly
> 5. End with a soft question

**Tweet 6:**
> The frame across all 5 parts: price for terms.
>
> You're not asking for a discount. You're asking the seller to trade something they don't need (the loan staying in their name briefly) for something they do need (full asking, fast close, no carrying cost).
