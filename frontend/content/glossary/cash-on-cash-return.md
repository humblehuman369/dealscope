# What Is Cash-on-Cash Return? The Investor Yield on Your Down Payment

Cash-on-cash return is the annual return on the cash *you actually put in* — not the price of the property. It is the metric that captures the leverage effect of using a mortgage.

## The 30-second definition

```
cash-on-cash = annual_cash_flow / total_cash_invested
```

Where:

- **Annual cash flow** = NOI − annual debt service (mortgage payments)
- **Total cash invested** = down payment + closing costs + initial repairs

Cap rate ignores debt; cash-on-cash includes it. Leverage usually moves cash-on-cash *above* cap rate when the loan rate is below the cap rate, and *below* cap rate when the loan rate exceeds the cap rate.

## A real-numbers example

Same $400,000 duplex with $28,056 NOI (see [NOI](/glossary/noi)).

**Financing**: 25% down, 7.0% rate, 30-year term.

- Loan amount: $300,000
- Monthly P&I: ~$1,996
- Annual debt service: $23,950

**Annual cash flow**: $28,056 − $23,950 = **$4,106**

**Total cash invested**: $100,000 down + $12,000 closing = **$112,000**

**Cash-on-cash return: $4,106 / $112,000 = 3.67%**

In this case, cap rate (7.15%) is higher than cash-on-cash (3.67%) because the mortgage rate (7.0%) is roughly the same as the cap rate — leverage doesn't help much.

If rates drop to 5.5%, the same loan costs $20,440 annually. Cash flow rises to $7,616. Cash-on-cash jumps to **6.80%**. Same property, same price, lower rate — *that* is the leverage effect.

## What's a good cash-on-cash return?

The market rule of thumb most investors use:

- **8%+** — solid for a long-term rental in 2026 rate environment
- **10%+** — strong, typically requires below-market acquisition or high-rent market
- **12%+** — excellent, usually only on BRRRR or value-add deals where rehab unlocks rent
- **Under 5%** — only acceptable when appreciation, tax benefits, or principal paydown make up the difference

These numbers shift with interest rates. In a 3% rate environment (2020–2021), 8% cash-on-cash on a Class A property was common. In a 7% rate environment, 5% cash-on-cash on Class A is normal — and 8% requires a deal.

## Cash-on-cash vs. cap rate vs. IRR

| Metric | Time horizon | Includes debt | Includes appreciation |
|--------|--------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Cap rate | 1 year | No | No |
| Cash-on-cash | 1 year | Yes | No |
| IRR | Full hold period | Yes | Yes |

Cash-on-cash is a year-1 snapshot. IRR is the long-form picture. Both matter; they answer different questions.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between cash-on-cash and ROI?**
Cash-on-cash is a year-1 metric. ROI is typically a total-return-over-hold metric that includes principal paydown and appreciation. They're related but not interchangeable.

**Does cash-on-cash include principal paydown?**
No. Principal paydown reduces your mortgage balance — it builds equity but doesn't show up as cash flow. Some investors track "total return" as cash-on-cash + principal paydown rate; that's a separate metric.

**Should I refinance to improve cash-on-cash?**
Refinancing into a lower rate or longer term increases monthly cash flow, which raises cash-on-cash. The trade-off is closing costs and possibly extending the loan. The math is straightforward: divide refinance costs by monthly cash-flow improvement to get the breakeven point in months.
