# What Is the 70% Rule in Real Estate?

The 70% rule is a fast-math formula flippers and wholesalers use to determine the maximum price they can pay for a property and still profit after renovation, holding costs, selling costs, and target margin.

## The 30-second formula

```
MAO (Max Allowable Offer) = ARV × 0.70 − rehab_cost
```

Where:
- **ARV** is the property's value after renovation (see [ARV](/glossary/arv))
- **Rehab cost** is the all-in renovation budget including contingency

The 30% discount from ARV covers selling costs, holding costs, financing costs, and the investor's profit margin.

## A real-numbers example

A property has projected ARV of $340,000 and needs $35,000 of rehab.

**MAO = $340,000 × 0.70 − $35,000 = $238,000 − $35,000 = $203,000**

If the wholesaler can lock the property under contract at $203K or below, the deal works at 70% rule. If they have to pay $215K, the math gets tight (66% rule). At $230K, the math fails for most flips.

## What the 30% discount covers

| Cost | Typical % of ARV |
|------|------------------|
| Selling costs (agent commission, closing) | 8% |
| Holding costs (interest, taxes, insurance, utilities) | 4–8% |
| Financing fees (hard money points + interest) | 3–5% |
| Profit margin (target) | 10–15% |
| **Total** | **25–36%** |

That's where 30% comes from. Investors backing into 70% have implicitly priced in all four buckets. Tight markets (high competition for deals) sometimes accept 75% or even 80%, sacrificing margin for volume. Slower markets often demand 65% or lower for safety.

## When to use the 70% rule

- **Fix & Flip** — the original use case
- **Wholesale** — wholesalers contract at MAO minus their assignment fee, so the buyer still hits 70%
- **BRRRR** — many BRRRR investors target 70–75% of ARV minus rehab so capital can be recycled at refinance

## When NOT to use the 70% rule

- **Long-term rental** — the rule doesn't apply. Use cap rate, NOI, and DSCR instead.
- **Subject-To and seller-finance deals** — when the financing is creative, the math is different. The seller's existing rate or terms can absorb a higher MAO.
- **High-end markets** — the 30% margin assumption is based on typical mid-market rehabs. Luxury properties often have lower expense ratios but tighter buyer pools.
- **New construction** — construction costs and timeline don't fit the 70% framework.

## Why 70% — not 75% or 65%

70% became the industry standard for residential SFR flips in the 1990s–2000s when:
- Hard money rates were 12–14% (now closer to 10–12%)
- Holding periods averaged 6 months (now closer to 4)
- Target profit was $30K+ per flip on $200K–$400K ARV properties

In a low-rate, fast-turn environment, 75% can work. In a high-rate or slow-comps environment, 65% is safer. The number is a heuristic, not a law.

## FAQ

**Does the 70% rule include closing costs at acquisition?**
The standard formula does not subtract acquisition closing costs explicitly — they're absorbed in the 30% margin. Conservative investors subtract them: `MAO = (ARV × 0.70) − rehab_cost − acquisition_closing`.

**Should I use the 70% rule in a hot market?**
Most investors flex up to 75% in highly competitive markets to win deals — accepting a smaller margin for higher volume. Be careful: a 75% deal that runs over rehab budget by 10% becomes a money-loser fast.

**What's the equivalent rule for BRRRR?**
BRRRR investors typically target 70–75% of ARV minus rehab so the post-rehab refinance (at 75% LTV of ARV) can recycle most of the cash. See [BRRRR Method](/glossary/brrrr-method).
