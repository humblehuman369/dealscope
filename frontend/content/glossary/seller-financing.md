# What Is Seller Financing?

Seller financing is when the seller carries the note on the property — playing the role a bank would normally play. The buyer makes monthly payments directly to the seller (or a third-party servicer) instead of to a lender.

## The 30-second definition

The seller transfers title to the buyer. The buyer signs a promissory note and a mortgage (or deed of trust) in favor of the seller. The buyer pays the seller monthly until the note is paid off or refinanced. No bank is involved.

## A real-numbers example

A $400,000 house. The seller owns it free and clear (no existing loan).

**Standard sale**: buyer brings $80K down + closing, gets a $320K conventional loan at 7%, $2,128/month P&I.

**Seller-financed sale**: buyer brings $80K down. Seller carries a $320K note at 6.0% for 30 years. Buyer pays the seller $1,919/month. No bank, no appraisal contingency, no origination fees.

The seller earns a 6% return on $320K of "capital" they otherwise would have received as a lump sum (and would now need to redeploy somewhere else). The buyer gets a lower rate than the bank offers.

## Why a seller would say yes

- **Tax treatment.** A seller-financed sale spreads capital-gains tax across years (installment-sale tax treatment) instead of recognizing the full gain in year one. For a $400K sale with $300K gain, the difference is meaningful.
- **Higher all-in price.** Sellers often agree to seller-financing in exchange for asking price (or above), accepting the trade-off of carrying the note for the higher headline.
- **Predictable income.** A 6% note on $320K is $19,200/year of mostly-passive income for the seller. Many retired sellers prefer it to chasing yield in the stock market.
- **The property is hard to finance conventionally.** Failed inspections, rural locations, properties with permit issues — anything making banks nervous. Seller-finance bypasses the bank.
- **Fast, certain close.** No financing contingency. No appraisal contingency. Close in 14 days.

## Why a seller would say no

- They need cash at closing for another purchase, debt payoff, or living expenses
- They don't want to deal with collecting payments or worrying about default
- They don't trust the buyer's ability to maintain the property and pay reliably
- Their attorney advised against carrying paper for a stranger

The pitch is half persuasion, half qualification. Not every seller is a candidate.

## Common structures

**Full seller carry**: 100% of purchase price financed by seller. Rare; usually requires a strong relationship or unique property.

**Seller carry second**: buyer gets a conventional first mortgage; seller carries a smaller second to bridge the down-payment gap. Common in deals where the buyer is short on cash but creditworthy.

**0% second**: a creative variant where the seller carries a 0% interest second for a few years to make the deal cash-flow. Effectively a price discount paid over time.

**Wraparound mortgage**: the seller has an existing conventional loan and "wraps" their loan with a new note to the buyer. Buyer pays seller; seller pays the underlying lender. Higher complexity; legal review essential.

## The tax angle (installment sale)

A traditional sale recognizes the entire capital gain in the year of sale. A seller-financed sale qualifies for installment-sale treatment under IRC §453: gain is recognized proportionally as principal is received.

For a seller in a high tax year, this can save tens of thousands in capital gains tax. For a seller already in a low tax bracket, the benefit is smaller — but the higher effective price often still wins.

Sellers should always discuss installment-sale treatment with a CPA before agreeing.

## When seller financing fits

- Seller owns the property free and clear, or has substantial equity
- Seller has a strong tax incentive to spread the gain
- Buyer can offer a competitive rate (typically near conventional, sometimes better)
- The property cash-flows at the seller-finance terms

## FAQ

**Is seller financing legal?**
Yes, in all 50 states, with state-specific compliance rules around disclosures and (for owner-occupied buyers) Dodd-Frank loan-originator requirements. Investor-to-investor transactions are typically straightforward; consult counsel for owner-occupied scenarios.

**What rate does seller financing typically use?**
Market practice in 2026 is 5.5–7.5%, often slightly below or at conventional rates. Sellers carrying paper for a long-term hold (10–30 years) typically want 6%+; sellers willing to carry shorter-term notes (3–7 years) sometimes accept 4–5% in exchange for a fast close.

**What's the difference between seller financing and subject-to?**
Seller financing creates a *new* loan from seller to buyer. Subject-to leaves the seller's *existing* loan in place — buyer takes title and makes payments on the seller's mortgage. See [Subject-To Financing](/glossary/subject-to-financing).
