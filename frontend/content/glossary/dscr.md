# What Is DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)? The Lender's Safety Margin

DSCR is the ratio of a property's NOI to its annual debt service. Lenders use it to gauge whether the property generates enough income to safely cover the mortgage. Investors use it to qualify for loans without proving personal income — the property qualifies, not the borrower.

## The 30-second definition

```
DSCR = NOI / annual_debt_service
```

A DSCR of **1.0** means the property's NOI exactly equals the mortgage payment — zero margin of safety. A DSCR of **1.25** means NOI is 25% above the mortgage payment. A DSCR below **1.0** means the property can't cover the loan from rental income alone.

## A real-numbers example

A duplex with $28,056 NOI and a 30-year, $300,000 loan at 7%:

- Monthly P&I: $1,996
- Annual debt service: $23,950
- **DSCR = $28,056 / $23,950 = 1.17**

That's a passable DSCR — most rental lenders want 1.20 minimum. To get there, the borrower would either need to put more down (lowering debt service) or find a property with higher rent.

## What's a good DSCR?

Lender expectations by loan type:

| Loan type | Minimum DSCR |
|-----------|--------------|
| Fannie Mae conventional investor loan | 1.00 (income from property qualifies the property) |
| DSCR loan (most rental lenders) | 1.20–1.25 |
| Commercial multifamily | 1.25–1.35 |
| New-construction or rehab take-out | 1.30+ |

Below 1.00, most lenders won't write the loan unless the borrower brings strong personal income to qualify.

## Why DSCR loans matter for investors

Conventional financing caps an individual borrower at 10 financed properties. **DSCR loans** — also called "no-doc" or "asset-based" rental loans — qualify the property, not the borrower. The lender looks at:

- Property NOI (or in many cases, just rent vs. PITIA)
- LTV (typically 75–80%)
- Borrower's credit (usually 680+)
- Property condition

That's it. No tax returns. No W-2s. No DTI calculation.

This is how active investors scale past 10 properties without a corporate balance sheet. The trade-offs:

- Higher rate (typically +0.75 to +1.50% over conventional)
- Higher reserves required (often 6 months PITIA)
- Prepayment penalties common (3–5 years)

## DSCR vs. cap rate vs. cash-on-cash

DSCR answers *the lender's* question (will the property cover the loan?). Cap rate answers *the buyer's unleveraged* question (what does this property earn?). Cash-on-cash answers *the buyer's leveraged* question (what's my return on cash?).

A property can have a great cap rate but a bad DSCR if the loan is too aggressive. A property can have a marginal cap rate but a great DSCR if the loan is conservative.

## FAQ

**What if my DSCR is below 1.0?**
Most rental lenders won't write the loan. Options: (1) put more cash down to lower the loan amount, (2) find a longer amortization to lower payment, (3) re-tenant or raise rents to boost NOI, (4) qualify on personal income via a conventional loan.

**Is DSCR calculated on gross rent or NOI?**
DSCR is calculated on NOI. Some lenders simplify to "rent ratio" (gross rent / PITIA) — that's not DSCR; it's a related but more lenient metric.

**Do DSCR loans require a personal guarantee?**
Most do, despite "no-doc" branding. The asset-based qualification refers to *income proof*, not liability. Read the loan docs carefully.
