# What Is the 1% Rule in Real Estate?

The 1% rule is a fast-math screening heuristic for long-term rental properties: monthly rent should equal at least 1% of the purchase price.

## The 30-second formula

```
1% rule passes if:  monthly_rent / purchase_price ≥ 1%
```

A $200,000 property must rent for at least $2,000/month to pass the 1% rule.

## The honest truth: the 1% rule almost never holds in 2026

In the 2010s, when interest rates were 3–4% and home prices were lower in real terms, the 1% rule was a reasonable cash-flow screen. In the 2026 rate environment (mortgages around 6.5–7.5%) and post-pandemic prices, very few properties in major U.S. metros pass the 1% rule.

Where the 1% rule still works:

- **Class C and D properties** in lower-cost Midwest and South markets (Cleveland, Memphis, Birmingham, parts of Detroit)
- **Distressed acquisitions** at 50–70% of market — the discounted price flips the math
- **Seller-financed or subject-to deals** where the effective cost basis is lower than the purchase price
- **Below-market acquisitions** via off-market relationships

Where the 1% rule almost never works:

- Coastal California, Pacific Northwest, Northeast corridor, most of Florida, Phoenix, Denver — typical ratio is 0.4–0.7%

## What investors use instead

Modern investor screens have shifted from the 1% rule to:

- **Cap rate** — accounts for actual operating expenses, not just gross rent
- **DSCR** — directly answers whether the rent covers the loan (most rental lenders require 1.20+)
- **Cash-on-cash return** — the actual yield on invested capital

The 1% rule is still useful as a *gut check* on a 30-property short list. If a property is at 0.4%, it's a hard pass without creative structuring or significant appreciation thesis. If it's at 0.85%, it might pencil with the right financing.

## A real-numbers example

A $400,000 duplex rents for $3,200/month.

- 1% rule: $3,200 / $400,000 = **0.80%** → fails

The same property might still cash-flow if:
- Operating expenses are well below market average
- The investor puts down 35% (lower debt service)
- The property is in a strong appreciation market and the investor accepts low cash flow for long-term equity

The 1% rule alone says "skip." Cap rate, DSCR, and cash-on-cash will tell you whether the deal still works.

## FAQ

**Is the 2% rule still a thing?**
Rarely. The 2% rule (rent ≥ 2% of price) was a hallmark of bottom-of-cycle Class C/D acquisitions in 2009–2014. In 2026, properties hitting 2% are almost always heavily distressed, deferred-maintenance situations.

**Should I avoid properties below the 1% rule entirely?**
Not necessarily. Many properties below 1% still produce solid cash-on-cash with the right financing structure (seller carry, subject-to) or appreciation thesis. The rule is a screen, not a verdict.

**What about the 50% rule?**
The 50% rule says operating expenses average 50% of gross rent — useful for back-of-envelope NOI on small multifamily. Most actual deals come in at 35–45% expense ratios after accurate underwriting.
