# What Is ARV (After Repair Value)? The Number Behind Every Flip and BRRRR

ARV is the projected market value of a property *after* renovations are complete. It is the single most important number in any flip or BRRRR deal — every other calculation flows from it.

## The 30-second definition

```
ARV = projected sale price (or appraised value) post-renovation
```

ARV is not the asking price. ARV is not the current value. ARV is what the property will sell for (or appraise for) once the rehab is done and the property is in market-ready condition.

## How to calculate ARV

ARV is determined by **comparable sales** — recently sold properties in the same area, same condition, same size, same bed/bath count.

The standard approach:

1. Identify 3–5 recently sold comps within ½ mile, ideally same neighborhood
2. Comps should be **sold within the last 6 months** (12 max in slower markets)
3. Match on bed count, bath count, square footage (within ~10%), garage, lot size, and condition
4. Adjust for differences (a comp with one extra bath is worth more; subtract that delta)
5. Take the average — that's ARV

**Example**: A subject property has 3 bed / 2 bath / 1,800 sqft. Three recently sold comps:

- 1820 Maple St, sold $342,000, 3/2/1,820 sqft, similar condition — base comp
- 1745 Oak Ave, sold $355,000, 3/2/1,900 sqft — adjust down ~$5K for extra sqft
- 1902 Elm Dr, sold $328,000, 3/2/1,750 sqft — adjust up ~$3K

Adjusted average: ~$340K → **ARV = $340,000**

## Why ARV drives every flip and BRRRR decision

The investor's maximum offer is anchored to ARV via the [70% rule](/glossary/seventy-percent-rule):

```
MAO (max allowable offer) = ARV × 0.70 − rehab_cost
```

- ARV $340,000
- Rehab $35,000
- MAO = ($340,000 × 0.70) − $35,000 = **$203,000**

The investor offers $203K knowing that after $35K rehab, the property will sell for $340K. The 30% discount from ARV covers selling costs, holding costs, hard-money interest, and profit.

For BRRRR, ARV drives the refinance loan amount (typically 75% of ARV), which determines how much cash you can pull out and recycle into the next deal.

## What can go wrong with ARV estimates

**Stale comps.** A comp from 12 months ago in a market that has moved 10% is no longer reliable. Most lenders won't accept a comp older than 6 months.

**Wrong neighborhood.** A comp from "the good side of the highway" doesn't apply to "the other side." Walking the comps matters.

**Differences in condition.** A renovated comp at $340K doesn't predict your post-rehab value if your renovation is at a lower spec. Match the finish level.

**Optimism.** The single most common reason a flip loses money: ARV was overestimated by 5–10%. Run a conservative ARV and a "best case" ARV. Make decisions on the conservative one.

## FAQ

**Should I use a real estate agent's ARV estimate?**
A licensed agent who actively sells in the neighborhood is the best source. Beware of an agent who is also trying to sell you the property — there's an inherent conflict.

**Does ARV include lot value?**
For most resale properties, ARV is the all-in market price (improvements + lot). Land-value-only ARV is a separate exercise typically used for teardowns.

**How accurate are AVM-based ARV estimates (Zestimate, Redfin Estimate)?**
Within ±5% on stabilized properties in active markets. Less reliable on properties needing major renovation, atypical layouts, or in slow markets.
