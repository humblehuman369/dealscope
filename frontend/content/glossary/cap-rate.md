# What Is Cap Rate? The Investor Metric for Comparing Properties

Cap Rate (capitalization rate) is the single most-cited number in commercial and rental real estate. It tells you the unleveraged annual return a property would produce if you bought it for cash.

## The 30-second definition

```
cap rate = NOI / purchase price
```

Where **NOI** (Net Operating Income) is annual rental income minus operating expenses — but *before* mortgage payments and taxes on income. Cap rate strips financing out of the picture so you can compare two properties on the same footing.

## A real-numbers example

A $400,000 duplex rents for $3,200/month and has $9,800/year in operating expenses (taxes, insurance, maintenance, vacancy, management).

- Annual rent: $3,200 × 12 = $38,400
- NOI: $38,400 − $9,800 = $28,600
- Cap rate: $28,600 / $400,000 = **7.15%**

A second property at $500,000 generates $30,000 NOI:

- Cap rate: $30,000 / $500,000 = **6.00%**

The first property has a higher cap rate, which means it generates more income per dollar of price. That doesn't automatically make it the better investment — appreciation, neighborhood trajectory, and tenant quality all matter — but on the income math alone, the first property wins.

## What's a good cap rate?

Cap rates vary by market, asset class, and risk:

- **3–5%** — Stabilized properties in expensive coastal markets (NYC, SF, LA). Investors accept lower yield for appreciation and stability.
- **5–8%** — Most U.S. residential rental markets. The middle of the curve.
- **8–10%** — Higher-yield secondary markets, smaller multifamily, properties needing some work.
- **10%+** — Distressed assets, tertiary markets, or properties with execution risk. Higher yield = higher risk.

Cap rate should always be compared *against the local market average*. A 7% cap is excellent in San Francisco and average in Cleveland.

## Cap rate vs. cash-on-cash return

Cap rate measures the property; cash-on-cash measures *your* return on the cash you put in. The difference is leverage. A property with a 7% cap rate purchased with 25% down can produce 12%+ cash-on-cash because debt amplifies the equity return. See [Cash-on-Cash Return](/glossary/cash-on-cash-return).

## FAQ

**Is a higher cap rate always better?**
No. A higher cap rate often signals higher risk — a property in a tertiary market, deferred maintenance, tenant instability. Compare cap rates within the same market and asset class.

**Should I use NOI before or after vacancy?**
After. NOI is calculated on *effective* gross income, which is gross rent minus a vacancy allowance. Most investors use 5–10% vacancy unless they have local data showing otherwise.

**Does cap rate include the mortgage?**
No. Cap rate is unleveraged — it ignores debt. To include the mortgage, look at cash-on-cash return or annual cash flow.
