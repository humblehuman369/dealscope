# What Is the BRRRR Method?

BRRRR is a five-step strategy that lets investors recycle most or all of their cash out of one property into the next. Done well, the same $50,000 of capital can buy 3–5 properties in two years.

**B**uy → **R**ehab → **R**ent → **R**efinance → **R**epeat

## The five steps

**1. Buy.** Acquire a distressed or undervalued property at a deep discount, usually with hard money, a private lender, or all-cash. Target acquisition: roughly 70% of After Repair Value minus rehab cost (the [70% rule](/glossary/seventy-percent-rule)).

**2. Rehab.** Renovate the property to a rental-ready condition. Typical hold during rehab: 2–6 months. Rehab budget is set at acquisition; contingency reserve of 10–20% on top is standard.

**3. Rent.** Place a qualified tenant on a 12-month lease. Stabilization typically takes 30–60 days post-rehab.

**4. Refinance.** Refinance into a long-term conventional or DSCR loan based on the property's [ARV](/glossary/arv) (not the original purchase price). Loan-to-value typically 70–80%. The cash from the refi pays off the hard money and ideally returns most of your initial cash.

**5. Repeat.** Use the recycled capital to buy the next property.

## A real-numbers example

A $200,000 acquisition (paid in cash or hard money), $50,000 rehab, projected ARV of $350,000.

**Phase 1 cash invested**:
- Purchase: $200,000
- Closing: $6,000
- Rehab: $50,000
- Holding costs (6 months): $4,000
- **Total cash: $260,000**

**Phase 2 refinance** at 75% of ARV:
- Refinance loan: $350,000 × 0.75 = $262,500
- Refi closing costs: $7,500
- **Cash returned: $262,500 − $7,500 = $255,000**

**Cash left in deal: $260,000 − $255,000 = $5,000**

**Capital recycled: $255,000 / $260,000 = 98%**

That $5,000 stays in the deal, generating monthly cash flow on a property worth $350K. The other $255K rolls into the next acquisition.

When the cash recovered exceeds the cash invested, investors call it **infinite ROI** — there's no remaining capital to divide cash flow against, so the percentage return is mathematically infinite.

## When BRRRR works

- The property can be acquired well below ARV (typically 30%+ discount, hence the 70% rule)
- Rehab is well-scoped and the budget is realistic (BRRRR fails most often on rehab overruns)
- Post-rehab rents support a DSCR ≥ 1.20 at the refinance terms
- ARV holds — comps don't move against you during the rehab
- Refinance lenders are open to seasoning periods of 3–6 months (most are; some require 12)

## When BRRRR doesn't work

- Cosmetic-only renovations don't add enough ARV to recover capital
- Rehab budgets blow up by more than the contingency reserve
- The market softens during the hold and ARV comps disappear
- The post-rehab rent doesn't qualify for the planned LTV
- Refinance rates rise materially during the hold, killing DSCR

## BRRRR vs Fix & Flip

Same first three steps. The split is at refinance vs. sell:

- **Flip**: sell on the open market, pay capital gains tax, walk with cash. Typical hold: 4–8 months.
- **BRRRR**: refinance, keep the property as a rental, recycle the capital. Typical hold to refi: 6–12 months. Long-term hold: 5–30 years.

Tax treatment differs significantly. BRRRR refi proceeds are not taxable (they're loan proceeds). Flip profit is fully taxable as ordinary income.

## FAQ

**How long is the seasoning period for BRRRR refinance?**
Most lenders require 3–6 months of ownership before refinancing at the new ARV. A few require 12 months. The seasoning period prevents flipping properties at inflated valuations.

**Can I BRRRR with conventional financing on the acquisition?**
Yes, but conventional loans typically won't fund a property in distressed condition. Most BRRRR investors use hard money, private money, or all-cash for the acquisition phase, then refinance into conventional or DSCR.

**What's the biggest risk with BRRRR?**
Rehab overruns. The single most common cause of BRRRR failure is a renovation budget that runs $20K+ over plan, eating into the equity that was supposed to be recovered at refinance.
