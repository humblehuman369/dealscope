# What Is a Hard Money Loan?

Hard money is short-term, asset-based real-estate financing — typically 6 to 24 months — used by flippers and BRRRR investors to acquire properties that conventional lenders won't fund.

## The 30-second definition

A hard money loan:

- Is secured by the property (the asset), not the borrower's income
- Closes fast — typically 7–14 days vs. 30–45 for conventional
- Funds properties in distressed condition that conventional lenders reject
- Carries higher rates and fees than conventional loans
- Is repaid in 6–24 months by either selling the property (flip) or refinancing into a long-term loan (BRRRR)

The trade-off: hard money is expensive but flexible. Conventional money is cheap but rigid.

## Typical terms (2026 market)

| Term | Typical range |
|------|---------------|
| Interest rate | 10–13% |
| Points (origination fee) | 2–4% of loan amount |
| Loan-to-cost (LTC) | 70–90% of purchase + rehab |
| Loan-to-ARV | 65–75% |
| Term length | 6–24 months |
| Prepayment penalty | None (most lenders) |
| Personal guarantee | Usually required |

A typical structure: lender funds 90% of purchase + 100% of rehab, capped at 70% of ARV. The borrower puts in 10% of purchase + closing costs + a small reserve.

## A real-numbers example

A $150K acquisition with $40K planned rehab, projected ARV $260K.

- Loan amount: 90% × $150K + 100% × $40K = $175K
- Loan-to-ARV check: $175K / $260K = 67% (passes the 70% cap)
- Borrower cash: 10% × $150K + closing ~$5K = **$20K**
- Interest rate: 11%, 12-month term, interest-only payments
- Monthly interest: $175K × 11% / 12 = **$1,604/month**
- Points (3 of 175): **$5,250 paid at closing**

Total all-in cost over 6 months: $5,250 + ($1,604 × 6) = **$14,874**

That cost is deducted from the flip profit at exit, or rolled into the refinance balance for BRRRR.

## When hard money beats conventional

- The property is in distressed condition (won't pass conventional appraisal)
- Speed matters — auction, short-sale, motivated seller
- You're past the 10-property conventional cap
- You don't have W-2 income for conventional underwriting (self-employed, full-time investor)
- The deal needs to close before due diligence finishes (rare but happens)

## When hard money loses to conventional

- The property is rentable as-is (skip the flip; do a long-term rental purchase)
- You can wait 30+ days to close
- You qualify for conventional financing — the rate gap (3–5%) eats most of the flip margin

## How to choose a hard money lender

The four questions that matter:

1. **What's their LTV/LTC structure?** Some lenders fund 70% LTC and require 30% from borrower. Others go 90% LTC + 100% rehab.
2. **What's the all-in cost?** Rate × term + points + junk fees. Always model the full cost, not just the rate.
3. **How fast do they actually close?** Some lenders advertise 7-day close and take 21. Ask for references.
4. **Do they finance rehab in draws?** Most do — funds are released as renovation milestones are completed. Make sure you understand the draw schedule before closing.

Most successful flippers have 2–3 lenders in rotation. Rates and capacity vary; relationships matter.

## FAQ

**Are hard money loans legal in all states?**
Hard money lending is regulated state by state — most allow it for non-owner-occupied investment properties without special licensing, but a few states require licensing. National lenders handle compliance for you.

**Can hard money fund the down payment too?**
Some lenders offer "100% financing" by combining a first lien and a private second. The total cost is significantly higher and lenders typically want significant skin from the borrower elsewhere. For most flips, plan to bring 10–15% of purchase + closing.

**What happens if I can't refi out of hard money before maturity?**
Most lenders offer extensions (30–90 days, with a fee). If you've stabilized the property and have a refinance in process, extensions are usually granted. Defaulting on a hard money loan means losing the property to foreclosure — these are non-recourse only when explicitly stated.
