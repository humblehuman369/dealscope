# What Is GRM (Gross Rent Multiplier)? The 30-Second Property Screener

GRM is the simplest valuation metric in real estate. Take the price, divide by gross annual rent, and you have a quick read on whether a property is in the right ballpark.

## The 30-second definition

```
GRM = purchase_price / annual_gross_rent
```

A GRM of 10 means the property costs 10 years of gross rent. Lower is better — lower GRM means more rent per dollar of price.

## A real-numbers example

A duplex listed at $400,000 generates $3,200/month in rent.

- Annual gross rent: $3,200 × 12 = $38,400
- **GRM = $400,000 / $38,400 = 10.4**

A second property at $500,000 generates $4,500/month.

- Annual gross rent: $4,500 × 12 = $54,000
- **GRM = $500,000 / $54,000 = 9.3**

The second property has a lower GRM — more rent per dollar of price. On the screener, it wins.

## What's a good GRM?

GRM varies wildly by market:

- **5–7** — High-cash-flow markets (Cleveland, Memphis, parts of Birmingham)
- **8–10** — Most U.S. cash-flow markets
- **11–14** — Balanced markets where appreciation matters as much as cash flow
- **15+** — Coastal appreciation markets where investors accept lower yield for long-term price growth

A GRM of 8 in California is unicorn-rare. A GRM of 8 in Ohio is normal.

## Why GRM is a screening tool, not a decision tool

GRM ignores everything that matters after the headline:

- Operating expenses (taxes, insurance, maintenance, management)
- Vacancy
- Financing
- Capital improvements
- Tenant quality

Two properties with identical GRM can have very different actual cash flow. A property with low taxes and good tenants might cash-flow strongly at GRM 10. A property with sky-high HOA and high turnover can lose money at the same GRM.

Use GRM to **screen** a list of 50 properties down to 10. Then run [cap rate](/glossary/cap-rate), [NOI](/glossary/noi), and [DSCR](/glossary/dscr) on the 10.

## GRM vs cap rate

| Metric | Top of equation | Bottom of equation | Includes expenses |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| GRM | Purchase price | Annual gross rent | No |
| Cap rate | NOI | Purchase price | Yes |

Note the formulas are flipped: lower GRM is better; higher cap rate is better. They're related but not interchangeable.

## FAQ

**Is a lower GRM always better?**
Lower GRM means more rent per dollar of price, which is good — but only on the income side. Properties with very low GRMs are often in markets with weak appreciation, high turnover, or significant operating costs that erase the rent advantage.

**Does GRM include vacancy?**
No. GRM uses gross potential rent (full occupancy). To include vacancy, use NOI-based metrics like cap rate.

**What's the difference between GRM and price-to-rent ratio?**
GRM is annual: `price / annual rent`. Price-to-rent is monthly: `price / monthly rent`. They're different scales of the same idea — annual GRM × 12 ≈ monthly price-to-rent.
